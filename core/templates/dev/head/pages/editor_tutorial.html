Welcome to the Oppia editor tutorial! This tutorial is meant to help you get started with your first exploration. First, we'll go over basic definitions and best practices, and then we'll walk through the process of making a small exploration.

The Process of Creating an Exploration

Think of an exploration as a conversation with the learner. You might start by introducing the topic you want to explain/explore, and then try and get the learner to engage with it. Along the way, you can ask questions to help the learner check their understanding, and provide them with appropriate feedback so that they don't get frustrated.

What should an exploration be about?

Typically, an exploration revolves around a question -- usually, the more specific, the better. A few examples:

  • What is the slope of a line?
  • What are the nutritional benefits of a banana?
  • What is the difference between Python and C#?

Note that the exploration does not need to go in depth or cover all aspects of what you are trying to teach. In fact, it's usually better and easier to make something basic (and non-branching) first, and then expand on it once learners start using the exploration. You'll be able to see the answers that learners are giving, and use these to add more cards/feedback as needed.

The Exploration Editor

When you first create a new exploration, you'll be taken to its editor:

The new exploration has just one card, called "First Card". Cards are the building blocks of explorations. You can think of cards as the places a learner (anybody who views your exploration) can potentially reach. All of your cards are visible in the Exploration Overview on the right. The editor interface only shows one card at a time, but you can navigate to other cards by clicking on them in the Exploration Overview.

Parts of a Card

A card consists of some content, together with an interaction and some responses that can give the learner feedback and take them to another card based on their answer. Here are the meanings of these terms:

Content

The editable block of text you see in the picture below. Write whatever you want the learner to read.

Interaction

This could be a text field or any kind of input field, such as a map. A learner will interact with these elements by entering some text or placing a marker on a map.

Response

Once an interaction is chosen, you can choose what happens based on what the learner inputs. You can also provide feedback, and then either have the learner redo the interaction or move them to a different card.

Improving the Sample Exploration

After you've gotten some learners to play through your exploration, you can revisit it, look at the answers submitted, and improve the exploration based on that.

For example, suppose you find that many learners give the answer 2 when faced with the second question. On asking some of them, you discover that they're computing (2 + 2)/(1 + 1). You can create an additional response that reminds them of the slope equation and that helps them identify the various terms in it.

In general, you can always go back and add new cards, refine the current ones, or check out answers that learners have submitted, so that you can improve the exploration further.
